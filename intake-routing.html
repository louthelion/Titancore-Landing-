Intake & Routing

Capture every request
and send it to the right place.

Intake & Routing explains how customer requests, forms, calls, emails, applications, messages, and inquiries should be captured, organized, and sent to the right company, department, or review path.

A business can lose money and trust when requests are missed, delayed, sent to the wrong person, or handled without a clear process. Intake & Routing creates order from the first customer contact.

Main Definition

Intake & Routing means controlling the first step of every request.

What is Intake?

Intake is the process of collecting information when a customer, partner, vendor, employee, or business contact reaches out. Intake may come from a website form, phone call, email, chat message, application, document upload, appointment request, service question, or internal company request.

Good intake captures the right information the first time so the business can understand what the person needs, where the request belongs, how urgent it is, and what should happen next.

What is Routing?

Routing is the process of sending the request to the correct destination. A request may need to go to sales, customer service, funding intake, underwriting, real estate review, property management, care coordination, administration, leadership approval, or another department.

Routing prevents confusion. It makes sure the right team sees the request, the right information is attached, and the next step is not delayed.

Intake Control Areas

A strong intake process collects the right information before work starts.

The business should not begin work with missing information. Every request should be captured in a clean, organized way so the team can understand who the request is from, what is needed, how urgent it is, and where it should go.

Contact information Capture name, phone, email, company name, location, and preferred contact method.
Request type Identify whether the request is sales, service, support, funding, property, care, vendor, internal, or leadership-related.
Customer need Understand what the person is asking for, what problem they want solved, and what result they expect.
Urgency level Separate routine requests from urgent issues, approvals, complaints, deadlines, and time-sensitive needs.
Documents attached Track files, screenshots, applications, contracts, photos, statements, forms, or supporting records.
Next action Define whether the request needs follow-up, review, approval, scheduling, quote, research, or escalation.
Routing Review

Routing should move the request to the right path without delay.

Company Routing

Company Routing decides which company or business area should receive the request. This matters when the public website or main contact channel receives different types of inquiries.

  • Identify which company the request belongs to.
  • Separate public questions from service requests.
  • Send company-specific work to the correct company page or team.
  • Keep holding-company information separate from company service work.

Department Routing

Department Routing sends the request to the department that can actually handle it. A funding question, real estate question, HR issue, customer message, or vendor request should not all go to the same place.

  • Route requests by department responsibility.
  • Attach the intake information before sending.
  • Mark the status so leadership can see what happened.
  • Prevent requests from sitting in the wrong inbox.

Leadership Routing

Some requests need leadership review before action. These may include approvals, contracts, complaints, exceptions, sensitive issues, payments, or major business decisions.

  • Identify requests that require approval.
  • Escalate urgent or sensitive items.
  • Keep records of who reviewed the request.
  • Make sure final decisions are not skipped.
Status Tracking

Every request needs a status so nothing gets lost.

Intake and routing are not complete unless the business knows what happened after the request arrived. A simple status system helps leadership see what is new, what is assigned, what is waiting, what is delayed, and what has been completed.

New The request arrived but has not been reviewed yet.
Assigned The request has been sent to the correct company, department, or person.
Waiting The business is waiting on customer information, documents, approval, or response.
In review The request is being checked, researched, priced, approved, or prepared.
Completed The request was handled and the next step was finished.
Escalated The request needs higher review because of urgency, risk, complaint, or leadership approval.

Business Request Types

Different request types need different routing rules.

A business should not treat every message the same way. A general question may only need a simple response, but a contract, application, complaint, vendor issue, funding request, or customer service problem needs a clear route.

Public Inquiry

Public inquiries include general questions from visitors, partners, vendors, investors, community contacts, and people learning about the company.

  • Capture the reason for contact.
  • Send general questions to the proper contact path.
  • Do not mix public questions with private internal work.
  • Route company-specific requests to the correct company page.

Service Request

Service requests should go to the business area that can help the customer. The intake should identify the service type, urgency, documents, and next step.

  • Funding request
  • Real estate request
  • Care coordination request
  • Auto or marine service request

Internal Request

Internal requests may include employee questions, approval needs, report submissions, document reviews, leadership messages, and company-to-company communication.

  • Route internal messages separately.
  • Attach reports or documents.
  • Send approval items to leadership review.
  • Track the status until completion.
Intake & Routing Flow

A clean routing flow protects the whole business.

02
Classify Identify the request type, urgency, company, department, documents, and next step.
03
Route Send the request to the correct company, department, person, or approval path.
04
Track Monitor the request status until it is completed, escalated, or closed.
Intake Questions

Questions every intake process should answer.

Who sent the request? Capture the name, contact information, company name, and preferred communication method.
What do they need? Understand the request clearly before sending it to the wrong place.
Where does it belong? Choose the correct company, department, team, or approval route.
How urgent is it? Separate routine requests from urgent issues, deadlines, complaints, or leadership items.
What is missing? Check whether forms, documents, photos, applications, or details are incomplete.
Who follows up? Assign responsibility so the customer or internal sender is not left waiting.
