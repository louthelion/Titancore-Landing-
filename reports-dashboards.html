Reports & Dashboards

Reports and dashboards help leaders see
what is working, delayed, or needs attention.

Reports & Dashboards help a business turn information into visibility. They show activity, performance, problems, progress, delays, costs, leads, conversions, follow-up, and the areas that need leadership review.

A strong dashboard does not only show numbers. It helps leaders understand what the numbers mean, what action may be needed, and what should be watched next.

Main Definition

Reports explain what happened. Dashboards show what is happening now.

What Reports Mean

Reports are organized summaries that explain business activity over a period of time. A report can show what was completed, what is delayed, what changed, what improved, what declined, and what needs leadership attention.

Reports help leaders understand the story behind the work. They turn tasks, numbers, customer movement, department updates, and results into clear information that can be reviewed.

What Dashboards Mean

Dashboards are visual control pages that help leaders see current status quickly. They can show open requests, pending approvals, lead counts, customer messages, department activity, financial movement, risk alerts, and incomplete work.

A dashboard should help people know what is active, what is late, what is ready, what is blocked, and what needs action.

Report Categories

Reports should be organized by the type of information leaders need to review.

A report becomes useful when it is clear, consistent, and connected to a decision. It should not be random information. It should help leadership understand status and next steps.

Activity reports Show what work happened, what was completed, and what is still open.
Performance reports Show leads, conversions, revenue movement, customer activity, speed, and output.
Delay reports Show what is late, blocked, missing, waiting for approval, or waiting for customer response.
Risk reports Show problems, warning signs, complaints, rising costs, weak conversion, or repeated mistakes.
Financial reports Show cost, revenue, cash movement, spending pressure, margin, and financial trends.
Leadership reports Show the highest-priority items that need management review or final decision.
Dashboard Review

A dashboard should make business status easy to see.

Current Status

A dashboard should show what is happening now. It should make it easy to see active work, open requests, pending items, overdue tasks, and completed items.

  • Open work and completed work.
  • Pending approvals and blocked items.
  • Customer requests and messages.
  • Department activity and ownership.

Performance Visibility

A dashboard should help leaders see whether the business is improving, slowing down, or missing important targets.

  • Lead activity.
  • Conversion movement.
  • Follow-up speed.
  • Revenue and cost direction.

Problem Alerts

A dashboard should highlight warning signs before they become bigger problems. Leaders should not have to search too long to see what needs attention.

  • Delayed tasks.
  • Repeated complaints.
  • Missing reports.
  • Rising cost or weak results.
Dashboard Modules

A strong dashboard separates information into clear control areas.

Each dashboard section should answer a business question. The goal is not to fill the page with numbers. The goal is to make the business easier to manage.

Lead queue Shows new leads, source, status, follow-up timing, and next action.
Pending approvals Shows items waiting for review, signature, correction, or leadership decision.
Customer messages Shows requests, questions, complaints, and messages that need response.
Task status Shows active work, assigned work, delayed work, and completed work.
Report center Shows daily, weekly, monthly, department, company, and leadership reports.
Alert panel Shows warning signs, overdue items, risk notes, and urgent review needs.

Report Review Types

Reports should help leaders review the business from different angles.

A business should not depend on only one report. Different reports explain different parts of the business and help leadership see the full picture.

Daily Review

Daily review helps the business understand what happened today, what is still open, and what needs attention before tomorrow.

  • New requests received.
  • Tasks completed.
  • Items still waiting.
  • Urgent problems or delays.

Weekly Review

Weekly review helps leadership see patterns, progress, repeated delays, customer movement, and department performance.

  • Weekly lead and customer movement.
  • Department progress.
  • Report gaps or missing updates.
  • Items needing leadership review.

Monthly Review

Monthly review helps leadership study bigger patterns, financial movement, growth direction, risk, and strategic decisions.

  • Monthly performance trends.
  • Cost and revenue movement.
  • Growth and risk review.
  • Next month priorities.
Reports & Dashboards Flow

A clean reporting flow collects, organizes, reviews, and decides.

01
Collect Gather activity, numbers, messages, tasks, reports, approvals, and updates.
02
Organize Place information into clear categories: status, performance, delay, risk, and next steps.
03
Review Leadership reviews what is working, what is delayed, and what needs correction.
04
Decide Use the dashboard and reports to choose action, correction, approval, or follow-up.
Dashboard Questions

Questions every dashboard should help answer.

What is active? Show what work is currently open, assigned, moving, or waiting.
What is delayed? Show what is overdue, blocked, missing, waiting, or not moving.
What is completed? Show what was finished, approved, sent, resolved, or closed.
What needs attention? Show alerts, problems, complaints, weak numbers, or leadership review items.
What is improving? Show stronger performance, faster response, better conversion, or cleaner workflow.
What should happen next? Show the next action, owner, deadline, approval, correction, or follow-up.
