Business Cost Definition

Business costs show how expensive it is to keep the company moving.

Business costs are the money required to operate, serve customers, maintain systems, pay workers, move products, keep locations open, and deliver work properly.

Labor CostsWages, overtime, benefits, training, staffing, payroll taxes, and scheduling pressure.
Supplies & MaterialsProducts, inventory, tools, office supplies, packaging, and materials needed for work.
Insurance CostsBusiness insurance, vehicle insurance, property coverage, liability, and risk protection.
Rent & PropertyOffice, warehouse, store, storage, maintenance, security, and property expenses.
Utilities & EnergyElectricity, gas, water, internet, phone, fuel, and energy-related expenses.
Shipping & LogisticsDelivery, freight, transportation, fulfillment, warehouse movement, and vendor delays.

Cost Warning Signs

Business cost pressure can weaken profit before sales fall.

A company can look active from the outside while the inside is under pressure. Leaders should watch costs, cash flow, margins, and customer demand together.

Margins get smallerRevenue may continue, but profit falls because expenses are rising faster.
Supplies cost moreInventory, materials, tools, and products become harder to price correctly.
Labor pressure risesOvertime, staffing shortages, wage pressure, and scheduling problems increase costs.
Shipping becomes expensiveFreight, delivery, fulfillment, and vendor delays affect customer service.
Insurance risesProtection costs can pressure real estate, vehicles, operations, and contracts.
Utilities increaseEnergy, phone, internet, gas, and facility expenses can reduce cash flow.
Pricing becomes difficultBusinesses may need to raise prices, but customers may resist.
Cash flow tightensHigher expenses can reduce flexibility, slow growth, and delay decisions.
