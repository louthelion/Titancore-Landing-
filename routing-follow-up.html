Routing & Follow-Up

Routing & Follow-Up

Routing & Follow-Up explain how requests should be directed to the right page, inbox, department, team, company, system, reviewer, or next step after information enters the business.

A strong routing process prevents requests from getting lost. A strong follow-up process makes sure the business responds, checks missing information, tracks open items, and keeps work moving until it is complete.

Back Home ›
Main Definition

Routing sends work to the right place. Follow-up makes sure it does not stop there.

Routing is the movement of a request after intake. Once a form, call, email, message, document, or application enters the business, the system must decide where it belongs. It may go to a company, department, inbox, dashboard, review path, approval path, records folder, or follow-up list.

Follow-up is the control that happens after routing. It checks whether the request was received, whether someone owns the next step, whether information is missing, whether a response is needed, and whether the work is still open, delayed, corrected, approved, or completed.

Routing creates direction It makes sure the request moves to the right company, department, page, inbox, or reviewer.
Follow-up creates movement It keeps the request from sitting without a response, review, correction, or completion.
Routing protects ownership The business should know who is responsible for the next step.
Follow-up protects trust Customers, partners, and internal teams should not feel ignored after submitting information.

Routing Destinations

A request should not move randomly. It should move to the correct destination.

Route 01

Company Route

Send the request to the correct company path, such as TitanCore, Vaultara, Family First, VitalPath, or RevMotion.

Route 02

Department Route

Send the request to the department responsible for reviewing, answering, processing, or completing it.

Route 03

Inbox Route

Send the request to the correct inbox, such as public contact, internal admin, leadership review, or support.

Route 04

Dashboard Route

Send the item to the proper dashboard queue so it can be tracked, assigned, reviewed, and reported.

Route 05

Review Route

Send the item to the person or role responsible for checking accuracy, completeness, quality, or risk.

Route 06

Approval Route

Send work that needs approval to the proper leadership, company, department, or final review path.

Route 07

Record Route

Send completed information to the correct archive, folder, record system, or history file.

Route 08

Follow-Up Route

Send open items to a follow-up list so the business knows what still needs response or correction.

Route 09

Escalation Route

Send urgent, delayed, risky, or unresolved items to higher review before they become bigger problems.

Follow-Up Types

Follow-up keeps the request alive until the work is complete.

Customer follow-up Respond to the person who submitted the request and ask for anything missing.
Internal follow-up Check whether the responsible company, department, or person has taken the next step.
Document follow-up Request missing forms, signatures, attachments, screenshots, contracts, IDs, or support files.
Review follow-up Check whether the item has been reviewed, corrected, approved, or returned.
Approval follow-up Track work waiting on leadership approval, final review, signature, or completion permission.
Delay follow-up Identify requests that have been waiting too long and need action or escalation.
Completion follow-up Confirm the work was actually completed, saved, and closed correctly.
Record follow-up Make sure final notes, documents, approvals, and history are saved in the right archive location.
Routing Workflow

A routed request should follow a clear path from intake to completion.

Step 01

Receive

The request enters the business through a form, call, email, message, document, application, or public inquiry.

Step 02

Identify

The system identifies the company, department, topic, urgency, request type, and required next step.

Step 03

Assign

The request is assigned to the proper company, department, inbox, person, queue, or review path.

Step 04

Confirm

The system confirms the request was received by the correct destination and not left without ownership.

Step 05

Follow Up

The system checks missing information, customer response, internal action, approval status, and open items.

Step 06

Close

The request is completed, recorded, saved, reported, and closed only after the correct action is finished.

Routing Rules

Routing rules tell the system what should happen next.

A routing rule is a simple instruction that tells the business where a request belongs. Good routing rules reduce confusion because the system does not have to guess. The request type, company, topic, urgency, documents, customer need, or approval requirement can determine the route.

Request type rule A funding request should not go to the same place as a real estate request, care request, or general contact message.
Company rule A request should be matched with the company responsible for that service or business area.
Urgency rule Urgent, time-sensitive, delayed, or high-risk requests should be handled differently from regular items.
Document rule Requests with missing documents should go to correction or follow-up before deeper review.
Approval rule Work that needs leadership review should move into an approval path before final completion.
Record rule Completed work should move into the correct archive and report history.
Common Problems

Weak routing and weak follow-up cause requests to get lost.

When routing is weak, requests can sit in the wrong place, go to the wrong person, wait too long, or never receive follow-up. When follow-up is weak, even a correctly routed request can still become delayed, forgotten, incomplete, or unresolved.

Wrong destination The request goes to the wrong inbox, company, page, department, person, or dashboard.
No clear owner Nobody knows who is responsible for responding, reviewing, correcting, or completing the request.
Missing information The request moves forward even though required fields or documents are missing.
No response timeline The business does not know when follow-up should happen or when a request is late.
No escalation path Delayed or urgent items do not move to higher attention when they need help.
No completion check The item is marked complete without confirming that the work was finished and saved.
No record update Follow-up happens, but the notes and final status are not saved in the record.
No report visibility Leadership cannot see what is open, delayed, waiting, or unresolved.
Questions To Ask

Use these questions before routing or following up on a request.

Where did the request come from?

Identify whether it came from a form, call, email, message, document, application, or internal task.

What type of request is it?

Separate contact, support, service, application, document, complaint, approval, and follow-up requests.

Which company owns it?

Confirm whether the request belongs to TitanCore, Vaultara, Family First, VitalPath, RevMotion, or another path.

Which department should receive it?

Route the request to the team responsible for reviewing, answering, processing, or completing the work.

Who owns the next step?

Make sure a person, department, company, queue, or process path is responsible for moving it forward.

What information is missing?

Check missing fields, documents, notes, contact details, signatures, or supporting information.

When should follow-up happen?

Set a response window, reminder, callback, review time, approval check, or escalation point.

How will completion be confirmed?

Confirm the work is complete, saved, recorded, and visible in the proper report or archive.
