Market Search

Market research studies the environment around the business.

Before someone starts or grows a business, they should understand if the market is strong enough. A market may look attractive from the outside, but the real question is whether demand, pricing, timing, and location make sense.

How many people need this service?
Is the market growing, stable, or weakening?
What price range already exists?
Is demand local, regional, national, or online?
Is the business seasonal or steady?
What economic signals could affect the market?
Customer Search

Customer research explains who the business is serving.

A business should not only ask what it wants to sell. It should ask who needs it, why they need it, how urgent the need is, what they already use, and what makes them trust one company over another.

Who is the real customer?
What problem do they need solved?
How often do they need the service?
What makes them trust a company?
What makes them walk away?
What are they willing and able to pay?

Competitor Search

Competitor research compares what already exists.

Competitors show what the market is already accepting. They also show customer expectations, common pricing, service standards, weak spots, and areas where a new company may be able to do better.

Who are the main competitors?
What do they offer?
What do they charge?
What are customers saying about them?
What are they doing well?
What can a new company do better?
Opportunity Search

Opportunity search looks for gaps and future growth.

Opportunity search is about finding where demand exists but service is weak, expensive, slow, confusing, or missing. It also looks at new trends, local changes, new customer needs, and underserved markets.

What customer need is not being served well?
What complaints keep appearing?
What new trend is creating demand?
What local change may create opportunity?
What group of customers is underserved?
What service can be simpler, faster, or clearer?
Risk Signal Search

Risk research helps visitors avoid expensive mistakes.

Search Intelligence should include risk. Some business ideas look good until someone checks cost, regulation, insurance, competition, weak demand, customer complaints, or cash-flow pressure.

What permits or licenses may be required?
What insurance or liability risk exists?
Are customers complaining about the industry?
Are startup costs higher than expected?
Is the market already crowded?
Could economic changes reduce demand?
Source Checking

Good research depends on strong sources.

Visitors should compare information instead of trusting the first thing they see. Business research should review dates, credibility, context, official sources, customer feedback, and whether the information still applies today.

Is the source current?
Is the source trustworthy?
Is this fact or opinion?
Are multiple sources saying the same thing?
Does the information apply to this location or market?
What important information is missing?

Today’s research focus is demand: who needs the product or service, how often they need it, and what problem they are trying to solve.

Ask who needs this service.
