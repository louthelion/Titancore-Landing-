Market Research

Market research helps visitors understand if the opportunity is real.

A market can look attractive from the outside, but the real question is whether enough people need the service, whether they are already being served, whether prices can support the business, and whether timing is right.

Market research questions

  • Is the market growing, stable, or weakening?
  • How many people need this service?
  • Is there enough demand to support another business?
  • What is the average price customers already pay?
  • Is the market seasonal or steady all year?
  • Are customers actively searching for this service?
  • Is the market local, regional, national, or online?
  • What economic signals could affect demand?
Customer Research

Customer research helps visitors understand who they are trying to serve.

A business should not try to serve everybody. It should understand the exact customer, the problem that customer has, what the customer values, and what makes the customer choose one company over another.

Customer Type

Who

Identify whether the customer is a family, business owner, investor, contractor, professional, institution, or local consumer.

Customer Problem

Need

Study what problem they need solved and why the problem matters enough for them to pay.

Customer Trust

Decision

Understand what makes the customer trust, delay, compare, ask questions, or walk away.

Customer research questions

  • Who is the best customer for this offer?
  • What problem do they need solved?
  • How urgent is the problem?
  • What do they already use or buy?
  • What makes them trust a company?
  • What makes them refuse or delay?
  • How much can they afford?
  • What would make them choose this company instead of another?
Competitor Research

Competitor research helps visitors see what already exists.

Competitors are not only enemies. They are information. They show what customers already buy, what prices are normal, what promises are common, what customers complain about, and where a new business may have room to be better.

Competitor research should compare

  • Who the strongest competitors are.
  • What they offer.
  • What they charge.
  • How they present themselves online.
  • What customers praise them for.
  • What customers complain about.
  • What services they do not provide well.
  • Where a new company can be clearer, faster, more reliable, or more professional.
Risk Research

Risk research helps visitors avoid expensive mistakes.

Every business idea has risk. Risk can come from money, licensing, insurance, competition, customer demand, timing, bad contracts, weak operations, poor follow-up, or underestimating the true cost.

Risk areas to review

  • Startup cost and monthly expenses.
  • Licensing, permits, and registration.
  • Insurance and liability.
  • Contracts, leases, and payment terms.
  • Customer acquisition cost.
  • Competition and pricing pressure.
  • Operational complexity.
  • Economic or market conditions that can reduce demand.
Decision Checklist

Good research should lead to a better decision.

Research is useful only when it helps someone decide what to do next. A visitor should leave the research process with a clearer understanding of the opportunity, the customer, the risk, the cost, and the first smart step.

Before action, visitors should know

  • Who the customer is.
  • What problem the customer needs solved.
  • How the market is moving.
  • Who the competitors are.
  • What the normal price range is.
  • What risks must be controlled.
  • What systems are needed to serve customers.
  • Whether the business idea is ready, needs more work, or should wait.
