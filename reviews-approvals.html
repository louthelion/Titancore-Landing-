Reviews & Approvals

Reviews & Approvals explain how important business work should be checked, corrected, approved, documented, and completed before it is treated as final.

A strong review process protects the business from mistakes, missing information, weak decisions, rushed work, unclear responsibility, and final actions being taken before the right people have reviewed the information.

Main Definition

Review means checking the work. Approval means confirming it can move forward.

A review is the step where information is checked before a decision is made. It looks for missing details, mistakes, unclear requests, weak documents, wrong routing, incomplete records, or work that needs correction.

An approval is the confirmation that the work is ready to move to the next step. Approval does not mean guessing. It means the required information has been reviewed, the proper process was followed, and the work is ready to be accepted, filed, sent, delivered, signed, completed, or closed.

Review checks quality The work is examined for accuracy, completeness, clarity, and readiness.
Approval confirms movement The work is allowed to move forward after the proper review is complete.
Correction protects the process Work that is incomplete or wrong should be sent back for correction before final approval.
Documentation protects the record Notes, approvals, changes, and final decisions should be saved for future reference.

Review Path

Important work should pass through a clear review path.

Step 01

Receive Work

The request, form, document, message, report, application, or task enters the system and is recorded.

Step 02

Check Intake

The first review confirms that the required information is present before the work moves forward.

Step 03

Assign Review

The work is routed to the person, department, company, or leadership path responsible for checking it.

Step 04

Correct Issues

If anything is missing, unclear, wrong, or incomplete, the work is returned for correction before approval.

Step 05

Approve Movement

Once the review is complete, approval confirms that the work can move to the next stage.

Step 06

Save Record

The final approval, notes, corrections, documents, and completion proof should be saved.

What Should Be Reviewed

The business should know exactly what needs review before approval.

Customer or client requests Check whether the request is clear, complete, and routed to the correct place.
Forms and applications Check whether required fields, documents, dates, contact information, and notes are complete.
Documents and reports Check spelling, accuracy, formatting, missing information, supporting details, and final readiness.
Financial or pricing decisions Check numbers, assumptions, cost, risk, profit, timing, and approval requirements.
Outgoing messages Check tone, accuracy, recipient, attachments, and whether the message should be sent.
Final deliverables Check whether the work is complete, approved, saved, and ready before delivery.
Corrections and returned work Check whether the problem was actually fixed before the work moves forward again.
Leadership attention items Check anything that affects risk, money, reputation, timing, approval, or final decision-making.
Approval Types

Not every approval is the same.

A business can have different approval levels depending on the type of work. Some items only need a basic review. Some need department approval. Some need company leadership approval. Some need final executive approval before anything is sent, filed, signed, published, or completed.

Basic approval Confirms the item is complete enough to move to the next step.
Department approval Confirms the responsible department reviewed the work and accepts it.
Company approval Confirms company-level leadership reviewed the work before final movement.
Final approval Confirms the work is ready for final action, delivery, filing, signature, or completion.
Conditional approval Allows work to move forward only if a correction, document, or condition is completed.
Rejected approval Stops the work because it is incomplete, incorrect, too risky, or not ready.
Correction Rules

A good approval system must know when to send work back.

Sending work back for correction is not failure. It is part of quality control. If something is missing, unclear, wrong, weak, or not ready, the system should return it to the correct place before anyone approves it as final.

Missing information Required details, documents, notes, names, dates, or fields are missing.
Wrong routing The item was sent to the wrong department, company, person, inbox, or review path.
Weak documentation The record does not explain what happened, who handled it, or what decision was made.
Unclear decision The next step is not clear enough to approve movement.
Risk not reviewed Money, legal, tax, timing, reputation, customer, or operational risk needs more review.
Final proof missing The work is being marked complete without proof, final document, note, or saved record.
Questions To Ask

Use these questions before approving important work.

Is the information complete?

Check whether all required fields, documents, notes, names, dates, and details are included.

Is the work routed correctly?

Confirm the right person, department, company, or review path received the item.

Was the correct process followed?

Check whether the work followed the proper intake, review, correction, and approval steps.

Does anything need correction?

Look for missing information, wrong details, weak documentation, unclear decisions, or risk.

Who approved it?

Record the person, department, company, or leadership level that approved the work.

What was approved?

Be clear about whether the approval is for review, correction, delivery, filing, signing, or final completion.

Where is the record saved?

Save approval notes, documents, corrections, completion proof, and final decisions in the right place.

What happens next?

Define the next step after approval so work does not stop after being reviewed.
