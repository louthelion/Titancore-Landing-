Records & Archives

Records & Archives

Records & Archives explain why documents, notes, messages, reports, approvals, decisions, corrections, and final work should be saved in an organized way.

A business should not depend only on memory, text messages, scattered emails, or someone remembering what happened. A strong records system helps the business know what was received, who handled it, what changed, what was approved, what was completed, and where the proof is saved.

Main Definition

Records show what happened. Archives protect the history.

A record is any saved proof of business activity. It can be a form, email, call note, document, report, approval note, correction message, signed file, customer request, invoice, meeting note, or completion summary.

An archive is the organized place where records are stored so they can be found later. A strong archive helps the business review past decisions, prove what was completed, understand who handled the work, avoid repeated mistakes, and protect important company information.

Records create proof They show what was submitted, reviewed, corrected, approved, completed, or sent.
Archives create order They organize records so the business can find information without searching everywhere.
Records protect decisions They help explain why a decision was made and what information supported it.
Archives protect continuity They help the business keep moving even when people change, forget, or leave.

What Should Be Saved

A business should save the information that explains the work.

Record 01

Intake Records

Save submitted forms, contact details, request types, timestamps, call notes, emails, and original messages.

Record 02

Work Records

Save assignments, work notes, drafts, document versions, status updates, and task movement.

Record 03

Review Records

Save review notes, corrections, comments, missing information requests, and quality checks.

Record 04

Approval Records

Save who approved the work, what was approved, when it was approved, and what conditions applied.

Record 05

Decision Records

Save leadership decisions, final instructions, policy choices, risk notes, and reason for action.

Record 06

Completion Records

Save final documents, completion proof, delivery notes, closing notes, and final archive location.

Archive Structure

Archives should be easy to search, review, and understand.

A business archive should not be a messy folder where everything is dropped without structure. The archive should be organized by company, department, customer, request type, project, date, approval status, or final record category.

Company folders Separate records by company so TitanCore, Vaultara, Family First, VitalPath, and RevMotion do not mix together.
Department folders Separate records by department so the right team can find the right history.
Request folders Separate requests by type, such as contact, service, application, approval, report, or follow-up.
Client or project folders Keep all related documents, notes, messages, and approvals together.
Date folders Use dates so records can be searched by day, month, year, or reporting period.
Final archive folders Keep approved and completed items separate from drafts, corrections, and pending work.
Approval history Keep review notes, sign-off notes, and correction history connected to the final file.
Search labels Use clear names and categories so records can be found without guessing.
Records Life Cycle

A record should follow the work from beginning to completion.

Step 01

Capture

The record begins when a form, call, email, message, document, or request enters the business.

Step 02

Classify

The record is categorized by company, department, request type, customer, project, or urgency.

Step 03

Route

The record is sent to the correct person, page, department, company, or review path.

Step 04

Review

Notes, corrections, decisions, and approval checks are added as the work moves forward.

Step 05

Complete

The final result is saved with completion notes, approval proof, final documents, and closure status.

Step 06

Archive

The record is stored in the correct archive location so it can be found and reviewed later.

Why It Matters

Strong records help the business protect itself.

They prevent confusion People can check the record instead of guessing what happened.
They protect approvals The business can prove who reviewed and approved important work.
They support follow-up The business can see what was promised, what is pending, and what still needs response.
They reduce repeated mistakes Past corrections help the business improve the process.
They help leadership Leaders can review history before making decisions.
They support reporting Reports become stronger when the record behind the report is organized.
They protect customers A saved history helps the business understand what the customer requested and what was done.
They protect continuity Work can continue even if someone is unavailable because the record explains the history.
Common Mistakes

Weak records make the business harder to manage.

A business can lose time and trust when records are scattered, unnamed, incomplete, or saved in the wrong place. The purpose of an archive is not only to store files. The purpose is to make the business history easy to understand.

Saving files with unclear names Names like “document final new 2” make records hard to search later.
Saving only the final file The business may also need review notes, corrections, approvals, and decision history.
Mixing company records together Different companies should not have important records mixed in one confusing place.
Leaving records in email only Important information can get lost if it only stays inside email threads.
No approval proof The business should know who approved work before it became final.
No completion proof Work should not be marked complete without a saved record of the final result.
No correction history If corrections are not saved, the business cannot learn from repeated problems.
No archive discipline Records should be saved consistently, not only when someone remembers.
Questions To Ask

Use these questions when saving business records.

What is this record about?

Identify whether it is a form, request, approval, report, customer note, document, decision, or completion proof.

Which company does it belong to?

Separate records by company so important information does not get mixed together.

Which department owns it?

Know the department or workflow responsible for the record.

What date should be attached?

Use dates to support reporting, review, follow-up, and archive search.

Was it reviewed?

Save notes showing whether the record was checked, corrected, approved, or returned.

Was it approved?

Record who approved it, when it was approved, and what approval level applied.

Is this the final version?

Separate drafts, corrections, and final versions so people do not use the wrong file.

Where should it be archived?

Save the record in the correct folder, category, company, department, and final location.
