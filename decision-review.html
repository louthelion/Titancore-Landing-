Decision Review

Use information to support action
before the business moves forward.

Decision Review helps a business use data, reports, customer feedback, market information, timing, cost, risk, and available options before choosing the next step.

A strong decision review does not rush. It compares options, identifies missing information, checks what should be tested next, and helps leadership decide what action makes the most sense.

Main Definition

Decision Review means checking the facts before making a business move.

What Decision Review Means

Decision Review is the process of slowing down before action and asking whether the business has enough information to move forward. It helps compare possible choices, review available data, identify missing details, and understand the risk behind each option.

Decision Review is not about delaying progress. It is about making sure progress is based on clear information, practical timing, responsible judgment, and a better understanding of what could happen next.

Why Decision Review Matters

A business can lose money, time, trust, or opportunity when decisions are made too quickly. Decision Review protects the business by helping leaders see what is known, what is unknown, what needs testing, and what should be avoided.

Good decisions come from clear review, not guessing.

Decision Review Areas

Every decision should be reviewed from more than one angle.

A decision may look good from one side, but still have problems in cost, timing, risk, customer need, competition, staffing, workflow, or long-term sustainability.

Data review Review reports, numbers, trends, customer feedback, sales activity, and past results.
Option comparison Compare different choices before deciding which one should move forward.
Missing information Identify what is still unknown before the business commits time, money, or resources.
Risk review Check what could go wrong, what could be delayed, and what may create loss or confusion.
Timing review Decide whether the business is moving too early, too late, or at the right moment.
Test review Decide what should be tested on a small scale before a bigger action is taken.
Decision Questions

A business should answer clear questions before choosing action.

What Do We Know?

The first step is to separate facts from assumptions. The business should review what is confirmed, what has been measured, and what has already happened.

  • What data supports this decision?
  • What customer activity has been seen?
  • What reports or results already exist?
  • What facts are confirmed?

What Is Missing?

A decision can be weak if important information is missing. The business should identify what still needs to be checked before moving forward.

  • What numbers are missing?
  • What customer information is incomplete?
  • What cost or timing is unclear?
  • What risk has not been reviewed?

What Should Be Tested?

Testing can protect the business from moving too big too fast. A small test can show whether demand, pricing, customer response, or workflow is strong enough.

  • Can this be tested on a small scale?
  • What result would prove the idea?
  • What result would stop the idea?
  • How long should the test run?
Decision Signals

Strong decisions usually show clear signals before action.

The business should look for signs that the decision is ready. If the signs are weak, the better move may be to test, wait, collect more information, or change direction.

Customer signal Customers show interest, ask questions, return, request service, or respond positively.
Market signal The market shows demand, space, timing, or a gap the business can serve.
Cost signal The cost is understood and does not create unnecessary cash pressure.
Risk signal The risks have been reviewed and can be controlled, reduced, or accepted responsibly.
Workflow signal The business can handle the work without creating confusion, delays, or weak service.
Growth signal The decision can support long-term strength instead of only short-term movement.

Decision Review Types

Different decisions need different review.

A pricing decision, service decision, hiring decision, marketing decision, customer decision, or expansion decision should not all be reviewed the same way. Each decision needs the right questions.

Business Action Review

This review checks whether the business should move forward with a new action, new service, new process, new offer, or new direction.

  • What action is being considered?
  • What problem does it solve?
  • Who benefits from the action?
  • What result should be expected?

Financial Review

This review checks cost, cash pressure, expected return, pricing, spending risk, and whether the business can afford the decision.

  • What will it cost?
  • How will it be paid for?
  • What return is expected?
  • What loss could happen?

Customer Review

This review checks whether the decision supports customer trust, customer need, customer experience, and long-term relationship strength.

  • Will customers understand it?
  • Will it solve a real need?
  • Will it improve service?
  • Will it protect trust?
Decision Review Flow

A clean decision flow reviews, compares, tests, and decides.

01
Review Study the data, reports, customer feedback, timing, cost, and risk.
02
Compare Compare options and identify which choice has the strongest support.
03
Test Test the idea when the information is not strong enough for a full move.
04
Decide Choose the next action with clear judgment, responsibility, and review.
Final Review Questions

Questions to ask before making the decision.

What action are we choosing? Define the decision clearly before the business moves forward.
What data supports it? Use reports, numbers, feedback, trends, and real activity to support the choice.
What are the options? Compare different paths before committing to one.
What is missing? Identify the information that should be collected before action.
What should be tested first? Use a smaller test when the risk is too high or the information is incomplete.
What will we review after? Decide how the result will be measured after the action is taken.
