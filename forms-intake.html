Forms & Intake

Forms & Intake explain how a business collects the right information before work begins. Intake can come from public forms, phone calls, emails, messages, applications, service requests, documents, or direct inquiries.

A strong intake process helps the business understand who is contacting the company, what they need, what information is required, where the request should go, who should respond, and what the next step should be.

Main Definition

Intake is the first checkpoint in the business system.

Intake is the moment information enters the business. If the company does not collect the right information at the beginning, the rest of the workflow becomes slower, weaker, and more confusing. Good intake prevents missing details, wrong routing, repeated questions, and poor follow-up.

A form is one intake tool, but intake is bigger than a form. Intake includes every way a request enters the company: website forms, contact pages, phone calls, emails, text messages, referrals, documents, applications, and public inquiries. The goal is to capture the right information and send it to the right place.

Forms collect structure They ask the same important questions every time so the business does not start with missing information.
Intake creates clarity It tells the business who contacted the company, what they need, and what should happen next.
Intake protects routing A good intake process helps send the request to the correct company, department, or review path.
Intake supports records The first record should explain what was received, when it arrived, and what information came with it.

What Intake Should Collect

A strong intake system collects enough information to begin correctly.

Field 01

Identity

Collect the person’s name, business name if needed, phone number, email address, and best contact method.

Field 02

Request Type

Identify whether the request is general contact, service interest, application, document, complaint, support, or follow-up.

Field 03

Company Direction

Decide whether the request belongs to TitanCore, Vaultara, Family First, VitalPath, RevMotion, or another company path.

Field 04

Need & Goal

Ask what the person is trying to do, what problem they need solved, and what result they are looking for.

Field 05

Timeline

Capture urgency, preferred deadline, event date, project date, appointment window, or response expectation.

Field 06

Documents

Collect any files, screenshots, IDs, forms, contracts, invoices, property details, funding documents, or support material.

Field 07

Consent & Permission

Confirm permission to contact the person, review the information, and route the request to the proper company or team.

Field 08

Next Step

Define whether the next step is a callback, email reply, review, application, appointment, document request, or routing action.

Field 09

Record Creation

Save the original submission so the company has proof of what came in and when it was received.

Intake Channels

Intake can come from many places, not only one form.

Website forms Contact forms, service forms, company forms, request forms, application forms, and document upload forms.
Phone calls Call intake should capture caller name, reason for calling, company needed, urgency, and follow-up contact.
Emails Email intake should identify the sender, subject, request type, attachments, urgency, and routing destination.
Messages Texts, social media messages, chat messages, and direct messages should be summarized and recorded.
Applications Applications need required fields, supporting documents, signature status, review path, and completion status.
Referrals Referral intake should collect who referred the person, why they were referred, and what follow-up is needed.
Documents Documents received without context should be connected to the correct person, company, request, or project.
Walk-in or verbal requests Even verbal requests should be written into the system so the business does not depend on memory.
Intake Workflow

A request should move through a clear intake path.

Step 01

Capture

The business receives the request through a form, call, email, message, document, or direct inquiry.

Step 02

Identify

The business identifies who sent it, what they need, which company it belongs to, and what type of request it is.

Step 03

Check Completeness

The intake record is checked for missing contact details, missing documents, unclear request type, or weak information.

Step 04

Route

The request is sent to the correct page, inbox, company, department, review path, or follow-up list.

Step 05

Assign

A person, department, company, or process path becomes responsible for the next step.

Step 06

Save Record

The intake record is saved so the company can review what came in, when it came in, and what happened next.

Good Form Design

A good form asks the right questions without confusing the visitor.

A form should be simple enough for a person to complete, but strong enough to help the business understand the request. The form should not ask random questions. Every field should help identify, route, review, or respond to the request.

Use clear labels People should understand what each field is asking without guessing.
Separate request types A funding request, real estate request, care request, auto request, and general contact request should not all ask the same questions.
Mark required fields Required details should be clear so the submission does not arrive incomplete.
Use short instructions Simple instructions reduce mistakes and help people submit better information.
Allow supporting details Notes, documents, screenshots, or extra explanation can help the business understand the full situation.
Confirm the next step After submission, the visitor should know what happens next and how the business may respond.
Common Mistakes

Weak intake creates problems for the whole business system.

When intake is weak, the company spends more time asking follow-up questions, searching for missing details, correcting wrong routing, and trying to understand what the person wanted. Good intake reduces confusion before it spreads.

Too few fields The form does not collect enough information to understand or route the request.
Too many random fields The form asks too much without helping the business make a decision.
No request category The business cannot quickly tell what type of request was submitted.
No routing path The submission arrives, but nobody knows where it should go next.
No ownership Nobody is clearly responsible for reviewing or responding to the request.
No record saving The submission is not saved in a clear place for future review.
No follow-up instruction The visitor submits information but does not know what happens next.
No correction path If information is missing, there is no clear process to request the missing details.
Questions To Ask

Use these questions before creating or reviewing an intake process.

Who is submitting the request?

Collect the person’s name, contact information, business name if needed, and best way to respond.

What type of request is it?

Identify whether it is general contact, service interest, application, support, complaint, document, or follow-up.

Which company does it belong to?

Route the request to the correct company or department instead of letting everything mix together.

What information is required?

Know which fields, documents, notes, dates, and details are needed before work can begin.

What is missing?

Have a clear correction path for incomplete requests, missing documents, or unclear information.

Who owns the next step?

Every intake record should have a responsible company, department, person, or process path.

Where is the record saved?

Save the original request and any supporting information in the correct location.

What should happen next?

Define whether the next step is review, reply, callback, document request, application, or routing.
