Automation Readiness

Automation Readiness explains what a business should prepare before adding automated routing, system controls, form movement, approval rules, follow-up reminders, or advanced tools.

A business should not automate confusion. The process should be clear first, so the system knows what information to collect, where to send it, who should review it, when it should be approved, and how the final record should be saved.

Main Purpose

Automation works better when the business process is already organized.

Before a business adds automation, it needs to define the real process. Automation can help move information faster, but it cannot fix a weak process by itself. The company must know the intake steps, categories, routing rules, review points, approval needs, record storage, and follow-up expectations.

The intake form should be clear before automation is added.
Request categories should be defined before routing rules are built.
The correct destination should be known before the system sends work anywhere.
Review steps should be clear before work is marked complete.
Approval rules should be written before the system triggers final action.
Records and system reports should be planned before information starts moving.

Readiness Checklist

What should be ready before automation starts?

Step 01

Define Intake

Decide what information must be collected first: name, contact information, request type, company, service need, timeline, documents, and next step.

Step 02

Define Routing

Decide where each request should go: public contact, company page, department, review team, leadership, records, or follow-up list.

Step 03

Define Review

Decide who checks the information, what must be corrected, what needs approval, and what cannot move forward until it is reviewed.

Step 04

Define Completion

Decide when work is complete, where the final record is saved, and what report or control item should show the result.

Step 05

Define Exceptions

Decide what happens when information is missing, the wrong department receives it, the request is urgent, or approval is delayed.

Step 06

Define Control

Decide what leaders should see: new requests, pending work, delayed work, corrections, approvals, completed records, and attention items.

Common Mistakes

Do not automate a process that is still unclear.

The biggest mistake is adding automation before the business knows the real workflow. If the process is unclear, automation can send information to the wrong place faster, create missed follow-up, duplicate work, confuse responsibility, or hide problems inside the system.

Do not automate forms that ask weak questions.
Do not automate routing without clear categories.
Do not automate approvals without written review rules.
Do not automate completion without saved records.
Do not automate follow-up without ownership.
Do not automate reports without knowing what leaders need to see.
Today’s Automation Readiness Focus

Define the process before adding automation.

Today’s readiness focus is process clarity. A system can only move work correctly when the business already knows the right path.

Check whether the intake form collects the right information.
Check whether the routing destination is clear.
Check whether review and approval rules are written.
Check whether final records and system reports are planned.
