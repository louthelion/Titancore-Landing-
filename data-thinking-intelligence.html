Data Thinking Foundation

Data thinking means looking for patterns before making decisions.

A business owner may feel that something is working or failing, but data helps confirm what is really happening. Data thinking helps visitors ask: What changed? What repeated? What increased? What decreased? What is missing? What needs attention?

Data thinking should answer

  • What information is being collected?
  • What numbers matter most?
  • What changed this week, month, or quarter?
  • What pattern keeps repeating?
  • What problem keeps showing up?
  • What information is missing?
  • What decision does the data support?
  • What should be reviewed before action?
Business Numbers

Business numbers show the health of the company.

Business numbers help explain whether a company is growing, slowing down, losing money, spending too much, pricing incorrectly, or missing important opportunities.

Money

Revenue

Revenue shows how much money comes in, but it does not tell the whole story by itself.

Cost

Expense

Expenses show what the business must pay to operate, deliver service, and stay active.

Result

Profit

Profit shows what remains after costs, but cash flow and timing still matter.

Numbers to review

  • Revenue by day, week, month, and quarter.
  • Operating expenses.
  • Cost to deliver the product or service.
  • Profit margin.
  • Cash flow and timing.
  • Average customer value.
  • Lead cost and customer acquisition cost.
  • Service completion time and response time.
Customer Signals

Customer behavior is also data.

Customers leave signals through questions, calls, form submissions, reviews, complaints, repeat business, missed appointments, delayed responses, and the reasons they say yes or no.

Customer signals include

  • How many people ask about the service.
  • What questions customers ask most often.
  • Where customers hesitate.
  • What customers complain about.
  • How fast the business responds.
  • How many leads become customers.
  • How many customers return.
  • What customers say in reviews, emails, calls, and messages.

Customer questions to review

  • Are customers confused by the offer?
  • Are customers asking for a service not listed?
  • Are customers complaining about price, time, or communication?
  • Are customers leaving before making a decision?
  • Are the best leads coming from one source?
  • Are customers repeating the same concern?
Market Signals

Market signals show what is changing outside the company.

A business does not operate alone. Market signals can come from competitors, prices, customer demand, inflation, lending conditions, housing, supply cost, labor, technology, and public business news.

Market signals to monitor

  • Competitor pricing changes.
  • New competitors entering the market.
  • Customer demand increasing or slowing.
  • Supply and material cost changes.
  • Interest-rate and lending movement.
  • Housing, construction, and local business activity.
  • Technology changes that affect the industry.
  • Public market or economic news that affects confidence.
Reports & Dashboards

Reports and dashboards help leaders see what needs attention.

Reports should not only look nice. A useful report helps someone make a decision. It should show activity, performance, delays, problems, trends, and priorities.

Activity

Volume

How many calls, forms, leads, tasks, jobs, clients, or requests came in?

Progress

Status

What is new, pending, delayed, completed, waiting, or needing review?

Decision

Action

What should leadership fix, approve, improve, stop, or watch next?

Reports should show

  • What happened.
  • What changed.
  • What is pending.
  • What is delayed.
  • What is improving.
  • What is getting worse.
  • What decision is needed.
  • What should be reviewed next.
Warning Signs

Data can show problems before they become expensive.

Warning signs are important because they help a business act before the situation becomes larger. A warning sign can appear in slow response time, lower inquiries, rising costs, repeated complaints, weak follow-up, or poor conversion.

Warning signs to watch

  • Fewer people contacting the business.
  • More customers asking the same confused question.
  • More leads but fewer paying customers.
  • Costs rising faster than revenue.
  • Work taking longer to complete.
  • More complaints or missed follow-ups.
  • Slow response time.
  • Reports not being reviewed consistently.
Data Decision Checklist

Good data should support a better decision.

Visitors should not collect data just to collect it. Data should help explain what is happening and what action should be considered next.

Before making a decision, ask

  • What data supports this decision?
  • What data is missing?
  • What changed recently?
  • What pattern is repeating?
  • What risk does the data reveal?
  • What do customers keep showing or saying?
  • What does the market suggest?
  • What should be tested before making a larger move?
